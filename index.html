Record your average temperature, rainfall pattern, soil type, and water availability. This information is the foundation for selecting a suitable crop.

  • Note months with heavy rain or drought.
  • Check soil texture (sandy, loam, clay) and pH if possible.
  • List reliable water sources (rain, well, canal, borewell).

Use the Crop Planner above to shortlist 2‚Äì3 crops that match your climate and season. Then prefer crops with moderate maintenance and local market demand.

  • Start with one or two crops only.
  • Talk to nearby farmers about yield and pests.
  • Check local prices and demand in the last few seasons.

List seed quantity, fertilizers or manures, pesticides (if required), irrigation needs, and labour. Estimate cost versus expected yield before sowing.

  • Calculate seed rate per acre and multiply by your land.
  • Include tools, machinery hiring, and transport costs.
  • Keep 10‚Äì15% extra budget for emergencies.

Level the field, remove weeds, and add organic matter if available. Follow recommended spacing and sowing depth for the selected crop.

  • Do primary & secondary tillage if needed.
  • Apply basal manure before final ploughing.
  • Use line sowing instead of broadcasting for easier weeding.

Monitor moisture, nutrients, and weeds weekly during growth. Schedule irrigation according to soil moisture and crop stage.

  • Thin excess plants for proper spacing.
  • Top‚Äëdress fertilizers at key growth stages.
  • Use mulching where possible to save water and reduce weeds.

Scout the field twice a week. Identify common pests and diseases for your crop and act early using recommended safe practices.

  • Prefer resistant varieties and crop rotation.
  • Use traps and biological control where available.
  • Follow label instructions strictly for any chemical use.

Harvest at the right maturity stage to maximize quality and shelf‚Äëlife. Handle produce gently and store under suitable conditions.

  • Harvest in cool hours (morning/evening) if possible.
  • Clean and grade produce before sale or storage.
  • Dry grains properly to safe moisture before storage.

Duration: 1‚Äì3 weeks

  • Time‚Äëintensive for a short period.
  • Includes ploughing, leveling, bund making, and manuring.
  • Mostly machine/animal power; plan labour in advance.

Maintenance: High

  • Frequent weeding and gap filling.
  • Close monitoring of germination and seedling health.
  • Light, frequent irrigation depending on soil.

Maintenance: Medium

  • Scheduled irrigation and nutrient top‚Äëdressing.
  • Weed control at 20‚Äì30 and 40‚Äì45 days after sowing.
  • Regular scouting for pests and diseases.

Maintenance: High

  • Moisture stress here can drastically reduce yield.
  • Balanced nutrition is critical.
  • Extra care to protect from pests, diseases, and lodging.

Frequently asked questions

Quick answers to common beginner doubts about starting cultivation and managing time and cost.

How many hours per week do I need to farm?

For a small plot (up to 1 acre), expect 10‚Äì20 hours per week during peak periods like land preparation and weeding, and 5‚Äì10 hours in calmer weeks.

Should I start with vegetables or grains?

Vegetables may give faster returns but usually require higher maintenance. Grains can be more forgiving but need more land and storage. Choose based on your time and local market.

Can I grow multiple crops together?

Yes, but for your first season, intercropping should be simple (for example: one main crop with one short‚Äëduration intercrop) to avoid complexity.
